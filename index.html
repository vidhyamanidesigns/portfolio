Zoom was built for business meetings. Students disengage within 30 min — no tools for interaction, participation, or post-class learning.

01 — Business Problem

Built for Meetings,
Not Classrooms

While Zoom is well-established as a video conferencing tool, its current design and feature set are optimised for business and social interactions. With the rapid shift to online learning, there is an untapped opportunity for Zoom to become the go-to platform for educational institutions.

Educators and students face challenges such as lack of integrated classroom tools (e.g. interactive whiteboards, attendance tracking), cumbersome scheduling, and limited support for engaging learning experiences.

Zoom is already trusted by universities worldwide — reliable technology, strong brand recognition, deeply embedded in many institutions. The global online learning industry is projected to exceed $400 billion by 2026.

06 — Usability Testing

Testing with Real Users

Goal: Evaluate the usability of three newly designed features with higher education students. Assess how easily they can navigate each feature, complete key tasks, and identify any visibility or usability issues.

👥

Participants

7 participants — undergraduate students and working professionals pursuing postgraduate studies, recruited through personal network.

🧪

Method — Maze (Unmoderated)

Conducted remotely using Maze. Each participant took approximately 3.5–4 minutes to complete all three tasks.

Findings & Iterations

Finding 01 — Quiz Try Again Button

Button state change not visible enough

🔴 High Severity

"Users did not notice the Submit button changed to Try Again after a wrong answer — instead repeatedly clicking answer options."

Before
  • Try Again used the same blue colour as Submit — no visual differentiation
  • Button state change was effectively invisible
After
  • 'Try Again' updated to red — clearly signals incorrect answer state
  • Error state is now unmissable and communicates consequence clearly

Finding 02 — Anonymous Toggle Visibility

Toggle lacked visual prominence

🟡 Medium Severity

"The Post Anonymously toggle lacked visual prominence — users took several seconds to notice it."

Before
  • Toggle was low contrast — blended into the chat panel
  • The key psychological safety feature was effectively hidden
After
  • Visibility improved by increasing contrast and enhancing visual hierarchy
  • Toggle now signals that anonymous participation is a first-class option

Finding 03 — AI Notes Follow-Up CTA

Follow-up CTA purpose was unclear

🟡 Medium Severity

"The 'Ask a Follow Up Question' CTA was unclear. Users also suggested viewing previous class summaries as a list."

Before
  • CTA purpose unclear — users didn't understand what it did
  • No way to access previous class summaries easily
After
  • CTA now opens a direct Team Chat with the professor, pre-filled with session context
  • Meeting history list added to sidebar for easy access to past summaries

Reflection

What I Learned

The hardest part wasn't designing the solution — it was staying in the problem long enough. Early on I wanted to jump straight to features. But it was only through research synthesis that the deeper structural issue became clear: Zoom treats every session as a standalone meeting with no memory, no course identity, no continuity.

My System Admin background helped more than I expected. I'm used to thinking in workflows and failure states. The three features I designed are meaningful improvements, but the real unlock would be a course layer: a persistent space that lives above individual sessions.

Biggest personal win: Learning to run unmoderated usability tests on Maze and interpreting both quantitative and qualitative data together to make confident design decisions.

01 — The Story

A Personal Frustration
Became a Case Study

I was shopping on AJIO one evening, four levels deep into Women's Western Wear, when I wanted to jump back to the home screen. There was no shortcut. No Home button. Just me, tapping back again and again, losing my place each time.

It was a small friction — but it happened every single time I browsed. That frustration became this case study.

I chose AJIO because it's my go-to fashion app — being a frequent user meant I experienced the navigation friction firsthand, not just as a researcher but as a real shopper.

When AJIO shoppers browse deeply across multiple products, they often get trapped in a back-tapping loop with no quick way out — turning an exploratory shopping experience into a frustrating one. On iOS, this is further compounded by UI patterns that don't align with native platform expectations.

The Smart Escape Menu is a contextual floating button that appears when users browse deep into categories. It gives shoppers a quick escape — whether they want to jump home, track an order, or scroll back to top.

🏠

Go Home

One tap to jump to the home screen from anywhere — no matter how deep you've browsed.

🔖

Back to Top

Appears only after significant scroll — contextual, not decorative.

📦

Track Order

Jump to order tracking without losing your browsing context — a frequent AJIO user need.

✦

Intent-Based Navigation

Full tab bar when exploring, a focused escape hatch when deep browsing. Context-driven, not always-on.

👤 My Role

01 — The Problem

AI Interfaces Optimise for Speed —
Not Trust

Claude and other AI tools have become daily companions for developers, researchers and knowledge workers. Users rely on them for coding, debugging, research and decision-making. While generation is largely frictionless, trust is not.

When AI responds fluently and confidently — regardless of actual certainty — users have no way to distinguish accurate claims from guesses. This creates a silent but serious problem: over-trust in AI-generated output.

Users do not struggle because AI makes mistakes. They struggle because AI uncertainty is invisible.

— Core Research Insight

02 — Secondary Research

Five Studies.
Four Design Implications.

Research synthesised from five sources covering trust in AI systems, human-AI interaction patterns, confidence calibration, and AI explainability.

🌊

The Fluency Trap

Streaming responses create false fluency — users find text convincing simply because it arrives fast and reads well. When AI mimics citation styles, perceived credibility rises even if the underlying logic is flawed.

Implication: Trust signals need to interrupt fluency at the right moment.

⚠️

Disclaimers Are Broken

Generic synthetic content labels have inconsistent effects. But when disclaimers move to the point of interaction — interrupting the automatic flow — they meaningfully change behaviour.

Implication: Placement and timing matter more than the warning itself.

📊

Confidence Is Miscalibrated

Traditional scores like "80% sure" are routinely misinterpreted. Optimal decision-making only happens when confidence values are calibrated to the user's own mental model — not a raw probability.

Implication: Raw percentages are not the answer.

🖤

Algorithm Aversion Is Real

Trust is not built on accuracy alone — it requires perceived transparency. For non-experts, aesthetics and the form of the explanation matter as much as accuracy.

Implication: Explainability is not a nice-to-have. It is the actual trust mechanism.

03 — UX Audit + Competitive Analysis

Claude Was Audited.
No Competitor Does This.

Claude was audited across five interaction areas using real-world prompts. The core finding: Claude communicates uncertainty through language, not through interface design. Whether Claude is highly confident or lacks information entirely, responses receive the same visual treatment.

Competitive Analysis — The Gap Nobody Has Solved

ChatGPT, Gemini, Perplexity, and Claude were tested with identical prompts. Key finding: no competitor proactively surfaces confidence signals or uncertainty in the UI. Perplexity leads on citations, but trust signals are still completely absent.

🎯

The Opportunity

Every major AI product communicates uncertainty through prose — never through interface design. No competitor proactively surfaces confidence signals. This is a clear differentiation opportunity for Claude to lead on trust and transparency.

4 out of 5 developers independently described the same unmet need — they wanted the AI to tell them what to trust, what to verify, and why — without any prompting from me.

— Interview Insight
🎭

"It looked correct but data was mismatching in production." "I trusted it because the explanation looked professional." — All 5 participants.

🔁

Cross-check docs, test in INT/UAT, ask seniors — completely unsupported by the interface. All 5 participants built their own verification process.

🏷️

4 out of 5 users described wanting a confidence signal — with a reason and source reference — completely unprompted. Users already know what they need. The interface just hasn't caught up.

⏰

"AWS and Azure info was outdated — caused confusion while troubleshooting." Version-specific and time-sensitive information emerged as a separate trust issue.

💸

47% either switched tools or reduced usage because of trust issues. Unresolved trust = user churn. Solving this = retention.

07 — The Solution

Clarity Mode —
Three Parts, One Note

Clarity Note appears only when Claude detects uncertainty, high-stakes information, or time-sensitive content. This preserves signal strength and prevents trust information from becoming background noise.

It does not appear on well-established, low-stakes responses — like explaining recursion. It appears on technical, version-specific, or rapidly-changing information — like a ServiceNow SQL query.

✅

Confident About

What Claude knows well — specific claims, established conventions, verified patterns. Green signal. Go-ahead.

⚠️

Worth Verifying

Specific claims to check before using — always claim-level and actionable, never generic. Amber signal. Caution without alarm.

🕐

Freshness Note

Version-specific and time-sensitive signals. "ServiceNow schema references are from my training data. If you're on Xanadu or later, verify DATEADD syntax against your current instance docs." Blue signal. Informational.

User Flow

4 Core Design Principles

Principle 1

Confidence, Not Certainty

Help users understand what Claude knows well without claiming perfection.

Principle 2

Verification Should Be Guided

Instead of "verify everything", identify specific claims worth checking.

Principle 3

Freshness Matters

Version-specific and time-sensitive information should be clearly surfaced.

Principle 4

Trust Without Friction

No new dashboards, no extra clicks, no complex scoring systems.

08 — Design Evolution

Lo-Fi to Mid-Fi —
Structure Before Style

5 screens mapped to the user flow. Greyscale only — no visual distractions. Focus on layout and hierarchy. Key decision: Clarity Note placed below the response and above action buttons — visible at the moment users evaluate output, without interrupting reading flow.

Design System

A complete visual language built specifically for trust communication — colours that carry meaning without relying on colour alone.

09 — High Fidelity Screens

Five States.
One Complete Story.

State 1 — No Clarity Note (Casual Chat)

Explaining recursion — well-established concept, fully confident. No note. Signal strength preserved.

State 2 — Clarity Note Expanded

ServiceNow SQL query — technical, version-specific, production-bound. All three sections visible: Confident (green), Worth Verifying (amber), Freshness (blue).

State 3 — Clarity Note Collapsed

Power users can collapse the note. Summary pills remain visible — 1 Confident, 3 to Verify, Freshness — so the signal isn't lost.

State 4 — Inline Verify Tags + Follow-up

Amber underlines on specific claims in the response. Tapping a verify tag opens a follow-up question flow — Claude explains why it flagged that claim.

State 5 — Settings

Settings → Clarity. Toggle Clarity Note on/off. Collapse by default option for power users — added directly from usability testing feedback.

Reflection

What I Learned

The most surprising moment wasn't a design decision — it was a research finding. When 4 out of 5 developers independently described the same unmet need — wanting the AI to signal what to trust and what to verify — without any prompting, I knew the problem was real and the direction was right. That one insight gave me more confidence than any metric.

What this project taught me is that transparency is a design problem, not just a policy problem. Every AI product handles uncertainty through disclaimers and fine print. Nobody has made it part of the conversation. Clarity Mode is my attempt to show what that could look like.

Designing for Claude while using Claude to help research and structure the project was a strange and rewarding loop. It made me think harder about what I actually needed from the tool — and that thinking became part of the design.
