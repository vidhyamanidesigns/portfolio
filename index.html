Case Study · UX Design · 2026

Zoom — Increase Adoption
in the Education Sector

Zoom was built for business meetings — not classrooms. This project reimagines Zoom's experience to address the specific needs of educators and students, driving adoption in higher education.

Project

Zoom for Education

Role

UX Researcher · UI UX Designer

Timeline

10 Weeks · 2026

Tools

Figma · Whimsical · Maze · FigJam

01 — Business Problem

Built for Meetings,
Not Classrooms

While Zoom is well-established as a video conferencing tool, its current design and feature set are optimised for business and social interactions. With the rapid shift to online learning, there is an untapped opportunity for Zoom to become the go-to platform for educational institutions.

Educators and students face challenges such as lack of integrated classroom tools (e.g. interactive whiteboards, attendance tracking), cumbersome scheduling, and limited support for engaging learning experiences.

Zoom is already trusted by universities worldwide — reliable technology, strong brand recognition, deeply embedded in many institutions. By evolving beyond "live video" and supporting purpose-built classroom experiences, Zoom can strengthen long-term adoption. The global online learning industry is projected to exceed $400 billion by 2026.

02 — Secondary Research

The Competitive Landscape

To understand where Zoom stands today, I looked at how BigBlueButton (built specifically for virtual classrooms), Google Classroom (focused on assignments and classroom management), and Microsoft Teams Education (a broader institutional ecosystem) approach digital learning. What became clear: digital education has moved beyond simple video calls. Universities increasingly look for platforms that support end-to-end classroom workflows.

Zoom's Key Gap Areas in Higher Education

🗂️

No Structured Classroom Workflow

Zoom works well for hosting live sessions but doesn't support academic routines like automated attendance, grading visibility, or course-level organisation.

📈

Limited Academic Analytics

Unlike LMS platforms, Zoom offers little insight into how students are participating — engagement levels, interaction frequency, or learning signals during a session.

🏛️

Weak Institutional Admin Controls

Administrators have limited visibility across classes, departments, and instructors — making it difficult to monitor usage or measure institutional impact.

🔗

Surface-Level LMS Integration

Current LMS integrations feel like add-ons rather than a seamless extension of the academic ecosystem.

🎯

Experience Built for Meetings, Not Learning

Many of Zoom's interaction patterns and UI flows are optimised for business meetings — which don't translate well to the needs of a classroom environment.

03 — Primary Research

Listening to the Classroom

I conducted user surveys and interviews — reaching out to higher education students and connecting with professors to understand their behaviours and pain points when using Zoom for online classes. I connected with 6 students and 1 teacher, keeping questions open-ended so participants felt comfortable answering honestly.

Although students are the primary focus, I also interviewed a teacher to understand how student disengagement impacts the educator's experience — specifically to validate "The Void" and understand the full ecosystem of the problem.

Key Insights

  • 01

    70% of students say classes should be more interactive

    The current passive lecture format leaves students feeling bored and anxious — too embarrassed to speak up and too disengaged to pay attention.

  • 02

    Students lose focus within 30 minutes

    Most students switch between apps during class — because disengagement is completely invisible on Zoom. Nobody notices, so there's no incentive to stay present.

  • 03

    Fear of speaking up

    Most students don't speak up due to embarrassment and lack of confidence — leaving them with unresolved doubts and causing the teacher to experience The Void when the class remains unresponsive.

  • 04

    Missing the presence of offline classes

    Multiple participants mentioned missing offline classes — not just because of limited interactivity, but because online classes lack the sense of presence and belonging that makes learning feel natural and human.

  • 05

    Teachers experience "The Void"

    Educators find it difficult to keep students engaged during online classes — having to manually initiate problems and discussions to break the silence.

Online classes fail not only because of limited interaction tools, but because they lack structured participation and social presence — causing students to become passive observers rather than active learners.

— Core Research Insight

Primary Persona

04 — Problem Statement

Naming the Real Problem

Higher education students in India struggle to participate in Zoom classes because the platform offers no structured way to make classes interactive — leading to students hibernating behind screens, teachers experiencing The Void, and ultimately putting Zoom's relevance in education at risk.

— Problem Statement
👥

Who is facing the problem?

Higher education students attending online classes on Zoom struggle to stay engaged and participate actively. Educators face difficulty gauging whether students are paying attention — often experiencing silent, non-responsive classrooms.

💡

Value generated by solving it

Better learning outcomes, increased participation, more confident educators, and institutional evidence that the platform supports meaningful learning — increasing the likelihood institutions continue using Zoom.

⚡

Urgency

As hybrid and online learning become permanent in higher education, platforms must support interaction and participation rather than passive viewing. If Zoom fails to address engagement challenges, institutions may prefer more education-focused platforms.

How Might We

HMW 1 — Increase Interaction

How might we make Zoom classes more interactive so that students shift from passive listeners to active participants?

HMW 2 — Reduce Participation Anxiety

How might we enable students to ask questions safely so they feel confident participating without fear of judgment?

HMW 3 — Support Post-Class Learning

How might we help students revisit key concepts after class so they leave with clarity instead of unresolved doubts?

05 — From Insights to Solutions

Three Solution Directions

Building on the insights from the user journey, I moved into solution exploration — exploring ideas including live quizzes, anonymous Q&A, automatic transcripts, participation dashboards, and low-bandwidth modes. From the brainstormed ideas, I selected three solution directions that most directly address the core engagement problems.

🎮

Interactive Breakout Rooms + Live Quizzes

Encourage active participation and reduce passive listening. Gamified points and leaderboard visibility keep students mentally present — shifting the classroom dynamic from broadcast to participation.

💬

Anonymous Chat Feature

Allows students to ask questions safely without fear of judgment — enabling anonymous participation directly within the existing chat. Reduces hesitation and encourages more students to engage during live sessions.

📝

Automatic Transcript & AI-Generated Notes

Students can revisit the class later, review important points, and resolve doubts even if they missed something. A Follow-Up Question flow allows students to directly reach out to the professor — ensuring continuous learning beyond the session.

06 — Usability Testing

Testing with Real Users

Goal: Evaluate the usability of three newly designed features — Live Quiz, Anonymous Q&A, and AI Generated Notes — with higher education students. Assess how easily they can navigate each feature, complete key tasks, and identify any visibility or usability issues.

👥

Participants

7 participants — undergraduate students and working professionals pursuing postgraduate studies, recruited through personal network. All are higher education students enrolled in college or university programs.

🧪

Method — Maze (Unmoderated)

Conducted remotely using Maze. Participants were shared a Figma prototype link and asked to complete tasks independently at their own convenience. Each participant took approximately 3.5–4 minutes to complete all three tasks.

Success was measured using Maze completion tracking (quantitative) and screen recording analysis (qualitative) — observing hesitation, confusion, missed elements, and unexpected interactions.

Findings & Iterations

Finding 01 — Quiz Try Again Button

Button state change not visible enough

🔴 High Severity — Needs Fixing

"Users did not notice the Submit button changed to Try Again after a wrong answer — instead repeatedly clicking answer options, suggesting the button state change is not visible enough."

Before
  • Try Again used the same blue colour as Submit — no visual differentiation
  • Users repeatedly clicked answer options not realising the state had changed
  • Button state change was effectively invisible in the flow
After
  • 'Try Again' CTA colour updated from blue to red — clearly signals incorrect answer state
  • Immediately distinguishes retry action from the primary Submit button
  • Error state is now unmissable and communicates consequence clearly

Finding 02 — Anonymous Toggle Visibility

Toggle lacked visual prominence

🟡 Medium Severity — Needs Improvement

"The Post Anonymously toggle lacked visual prominence — users took several seconds to notice it, suggesting it needs stronger visual emphasis to ensure students feel safe participating."

Before
  • Toggle was low contrast — blended into the chat panel
  • Users took several seconds to notice the anonymous option existed
  • The key psychological safety feature was effectively hidden
After
  • Visibility improved by increasing contrast and enhancing visual hierarchy
  • Easier for users to notice and activate — encouraging safe participation
  • Toggle now visually signals that anonymous participation is a first-class option

Finding 03 — AI Notes Follow-Up CTA & Meeting History

Follow-up CTA purpose was unclear

🟡 Medium Severity — Needs Improvement

"Users navigated the Class Summary and AI Notes without confusion — validating the layout. However, the 'Ask a Follow Up Question' CTA was unclear. Users also suggested viewing previous class summaries as a list for easier access."

Before
  • Ask a Follow Up Question CTA — users didn't understand its purpose
  • No way to access previous class summaries easily
  • Follow-up flow didn't connect back to the professor contextually
After
  • CTA now opens a direct Team Chat with the professor, pre-filled with session context
  • Meeting history list added to the sidebar for easy access to past class summaries
  • Follow-up flow is now clearly connected to continuous post-class learning

07 — High Fidelity Designs

The Final Designs

Live Quiz

The Live Quiz feature transforms passive Zoom sessions into interactive learning experiences. Unlike Zoom's existing polling system, it provides instant feedback (correct/incorrect), gamified points, and leaderboard visibility — encouraging students to actively participate instead of passively consuming content.

Anonymous Chat

The Anonymous Chat feature addresses a key behavioural barrier — students often hesitate to ask questions due to fear of judgment. By allowing anonymous participation within the chat itself, students can ask doubts freely, participation increases without social pressure, and classroom inclusivity improves.

Transcript & AI-Generated Notes

The Transcript and AI Notes feature ensures students don't leave sessions with gaps in understanding. It enables post-class revision, structured summaries, and easy recall of key concepts. The Follow-Up Question flow allows students to directly reach out to the professor — ensuring continuous learning beyond the session.

Design System

The Figma file was structured following design documentation standards — including a dedicated Design System page with colour tokens, component states, and reusable UI elements. All frames are clearly named and organised by feature.

08 — Design Rationale

Why These Solutions

😴

Problem 1 — Low Engagement

Students lose focus within 30 minutes due to passive learning formats.

Solution: Live Quiz introduces interaction, competition, and real-time feedback — keeping students mentally present throughout the session.

😰

Problem 2 — Fear of Participation

Students hesitate to ask questions due to lack of confidence and fear of judgment.

Solution: Anonymous Chat removes social pressure and creates a psychologically safe space for participation.

🧠

Problem 3 — Lack of Retention & Clarity

Students leave sessions without clear notes or understanding.

Solution: AI Notes + Transcript + Follow-Up flow ensures students can revisit, reflect, and clarify doubts — extending learning continuity beyond the live session.

09 — KPIs & Responsive Design

Measuring Success

Key Performance Indicators

How We'll Know It's Working

📊 Quiz Engagement

≥ 70% of students completing live quiz questions per session

💬 Anonymous Participation

5–10 anonymous questions per session — indicating students feel safe to participate

📝 Post-Class Engagement

≥ 60% of students accessing AI Notes or Transcript within 24 hours of class

Responsive Design

The current designs are optimised for desktop as the primary device for Zoom classes. However, responsive design principles were considered throughout — ensuring components like the quiz popup, chat panel, and class summary use flexible layouts that can adapt to smaller screens.

Mobile optimisation is planned as Phase 4 of the future roadmap — prioritising the 60% of Asia-Pacific students who access online learning via smartphones.

10 — Future Improvements

The Roadmap Ahead

Phase 1

Live Games

Short interactive games during class breaks to gamify the learning experience and maintain student energy throughout longer sessions.

Phase 2

Pre-Class Agenda

Helps students know what topics will be covered before class begins — reducing joining anxiety and mentally preparing them for the session.

Phase 3

Performance Dashboard

Tracks each student's participation rate and engagement during sessions — helping educators identify students who need support and reducing manual tracking burden.

Phase 4

Mobile Version

Mobile-optimised experience for all new features — addressing the research finding that 60% of students in Asia-Pacific access online learning via smartphones.

This redesign transforms Zoom from a passive communication tool into an active learning platform — improving engagement, participation, and learning continuity beyond the classroom.

— Project Summary

Reflection

What I Learned

The hardest part wasn't designing the solution — it was staying in the problem long enough. Early on I wanted to jump straight to features. But it was only through research synthesis that the deeper structural issue became clear: Zoom treats every session as a standalone meeting with no memory, no course identity, no continuity.

My System Admin background helped more than I expected. I'm used to thinking in workflows and failure states. The "meeting" mental model is Zoom's original design debt — and UI-level solutions only paper over it. The three features I designed are meaningful improvements, but the real unlock would be a course layer: a persistent space that lives above individual sessions and carries context, materials, and student relationships across a full semester.

Biggest personal win: Learning to run unmoderated usability tests on Maze and interpreting both quantitative (completion rate, misclick rate) and qualitative (screen recordings, hesitation patterns) data together to make confident design decisions.

Case Study · iOS Redesign · 2026

Breaking the Back-Tap Loop —
Rethinking AJIO's Deep Navigation on iOS

A focused iOS redesign that solved AJIO's deep navigation trap with one elegant interaction — the Smart Escape Menu.

Project

AJIO iOS Navigation

Role

UX Researcher · UI UX Designer

Platform

iOS Mobile

Tools

Figma · Apple iOS 17 UI Kit

01 — The Story

A Personal Frustration
Became a Case Study

I was shopping on AJIO one evening, four levels deep into Women's Western Wear, when I wanted to jump back to the home screen. There was no shortcut. No Home button. Just me, tapping back again and again, losing my place each time.

It was a small friction — but it happened every single time I browsed. That frustration became this case study.

I chose AJIO because it's my go-to fashion app — the online equivalent of Trends, where I shop regularly offline. Being a frequent user meant I experienced the navigation friction firsthand, not just as a researcher but as a real shopper.

02 — Problem Statement

Trapped in a Back-Tap Loop

When AJIO shoppers browse deeply across multiple products, they often get trapped in a back-tapping loop with no quick way out — turning an exploratory shopping experience into a frustrating one. On iOS, this is further compounded by UI patterns that don't align with native platform expectations, making the app feel unfamiliar and unrefined.

— Problem Statement

03 — Research & Audit

Hands-On Audit +
Competitive Analysis

Research was conducted through hands-on app usage as a real AJIO user, combined with secondary competitive analysis. Personal friction points formed the foundation of the audit, validated through systematic testing against a structured UX checklist.

Key Audit Findings

Area Finding Severity
Deep Navigation No shortcut to exit deep category paths — users must tap back 4–5 times to reach home 🔴 High
iOS Conventions Tab bar does not follow iOS HIG — icons, spacing and hit targets feel non-native 🔴 High
Navigation Visibility No persistent Home or escape CTA visible during deep browsing sessions 🔴 High
Back-to-Top No scroll-to-top shortcut on long category pages — forces full manual scroll 🟡 Medium
Tab Bar Labels Some tab bar icons lack clear labels — ambiguous for new users 🟡 Medium

Competitive Analysis

Three direct competitors were audited across five dimensions — Amazon, Nykaa Fashion, and Tata CLiQ. Two surprises directly shaped the design direction:

🏠

Tata CLiQ — The Root Icon

A small but brilliant detail — a root icon that instantly takes you back home from anywhere in the app. So simple, yet AJIO didn't have anything like it.

📱

Nykaa Fashion — iOS Native

Their iOS tab bar felt genuinely native and clean — proving that Indian fashion apps CAN embrace iOS conventions without losing their brand identity.

💡

The Design Direction

Tata CLiQ solves the navigation trap. Nykaa proves Indian fashion apps can feel iOS native. AJIO needs both.

Framing the Right Questions

05 — The Solution

The Smart Escape Menu

Ideation followed a divergent-then-convergent process — generating multiple solutions per HMW before selecting the most elegant and feasible direction. Paper sketches were completed first, followed by lo-fi wireframes in Figma.

Across all three HMW questions, one unified solution emerged that addresses navigation escape, visibility, and iOS consistency simultaneously. The Smart Escape Menu is a contextual floating button that appears when users browse deep into categories.

It gives shoppers a quick escape — whether they want to jump home, track an order, or simply start fresh. Rather than keeping a permanent tab bar that disrupts the browsing experience, a floating button preserves AJIO's existing aesthetic — unobtrusive when not needed, instantly accessible when it is.

🏠

Go Home

One tap to jump to the home screen from anywhere — no matter how deep you've browsed.

🔖

Back to Top

Appears only after significant scroll — contextual, not decorative. Every option serves a real user need.

📦

Track Order

Jump to order tracking without losing your browsing context — a frequent AJIO user need.

✦

Intent-Based Navigation

Rather than forcing users to choose between a cluttered nav and no nav at all, the Smart Escape Menu introduces a contextual system — full tab bar when exploring, a focused escape hatch when deep browsing.

06 — Design Decisions

Every Choice Was Intentional

  • 01

    Bottom-right placement

    The most thumb-accessible zone on a phone — requiring minimal reach for one-handed use.

  • 02

    White pills + blurred background

    Keeps products visible underneath without fully interrupting the browsing experience. The blur signals "you're still on the same page" — feels native to iOS.

  • 03

    Red dismiss button

    A universally understood signal for close — so users never feel trapped inside the menu itself.

  • 04

    iOS native tab bar

    Inspired by Nykaa Fashion's approach — aligned with iOS HIG conventions so AJIO feels refined and familiar on iPhone.

07 — Wireframes

Lo-Fi to Hi-Fi

Lo-Fi — Paper Sketches

Paper sketches validated the three-state navigation concept before committing to Figma. Three states were sketched: Home (full nav), Category Listing (floating button resting), and Floating Button Tapped (Smart Escape Menu expanded).

Mid-Fi — Figma Wireframes

Mid-fi wireframes were built in Figma using the official Apple iOS 17 UI Kit components. Real product data was introduced at this stage to simulate realistic browsing context.

Hi-Fi — Final Screens

Hi-fi screens were built with AJIO's brand colours and real product imagery. Five screens document the complete user journey.

09 — Reflection

What I Learned

The most impactful design problems aren't always the loudest ones. A simple navigation frustration — tapping back again and again — revealed a deeper gap in how AJIO thinks about its iOS users. Solving it taught me to design with accessibility and intention in mind, ensuring every interaction serves a clear user need.

Looking ahead, there's more to explore. The Smart Escape Menu is a focused fix — but AJIO's home screen tells a bigger story. Content, promotions, navigation and utility elements all compete for equal attention, creating cognitive overload on first load. A future iteration would address this visual hierarchy, giving users a calmer, more intentional entry point into their shopping journey.
